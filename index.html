Leibniz - Write Math, Not Code.

Would it be nice if we comunicate with computers in natural math language?

Have you ever dreamed of communicating with computers in the native tongue of mathematics, namely, natural math language when doing math using computers?

I know I have, as a matter of fact, for many years. And this is what this project is all about -- To develop an easy-to-use declarative language that maximally resembles natural math language with the constraint of ASCII for defining math problems -- numerical, symbolic, or even logical. Leibniz is a hybrid language that mixes English and symbolic expressions. The common structures it includes (assertion, command, and query) and the logical representation of math concepts and entities, are what give rise to its impressive express power.

Indeed, if MOOC is to hold to the promise of democritizing education better than previous hypes, it must SCALE beyond content delivery, especially in teacher/students interactions. It's a high order, but Leibniz can help -- question asking and easy-to-follow solution are just the tip of the iceberg of what it can offer!

Just as mathematics is the foundation for physics and engineering, among others, Leibniz language is meant to be the parent language from which domain languages can be derived with domain-specific lexicons and expanded grammar when necessary.

FAQ

How are you going to monetize this?

We plan on providing learning service to institutions with value-added functionalities such as homework composing and grading in both standardized form and simple-structured open form. For a brief introduction about how web-based homework assignment works, visit WebWork's maa site. Our service will send notifications to teachers/TAs when Leibniz fails to give students satisfying answers to their questions/problems such that they can get further assistances. If these services are proved saving time for teachers/TAs and allowing them to spend more time with students face-to-face in traditional campus, or improving learning experience of students in MOOC setting, there is money to be earned from these primimum services.

Another potential source of revenue is charging technical users who doing routine but tedious numerical work on a daily basis based on their actual use of Leibniz. Part of the fees will be used to pay the PaaS vender and the rest will go to us. In addition, if the users have powerful workstations or muli-core machines in their offices, we may install the software locally for them too, which can run from the same browser interface. "cloud computing" is the current trend, but we see no reason to abondon the computing harware one already has. Users who access Leibniz locally will be required to pay a traditional software licence fee.

What is the current status of the project?

Our development is somewhere in between "prototype" and "product", perhaps a little closer to the "product" end. The project was started in about five and half years ago, although the idea was formed during my graduate years at USC. Grammar definition of the language is mostly finalized and considerable implementation has been completed, including core numerical and symbolic libraries, and some simple logical programming functionality that enables Leibniz to perform mathematical reasoning in the context of single variable calculus. To our best knowledge, formal reasoning in calculus by software is something that nobody has ever tried before despite that reasoning is an essential skill math students are expected to acquire. Our numerical libraries include modules for in-core LU and QR decomposition of large sparse matrices.

But there are many tasks remain to be done -- the libraries as they stand now are very fragile and incomplete. The interpreter is mostly written in C++ with Microsoft's compiler, which makes it hard to cross compile on linux. Taking Dr. Balaji S.
