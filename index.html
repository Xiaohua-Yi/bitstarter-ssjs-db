Leibniz - Write Math, Not Code.

Would it be nice if we can comunicate with computers in natural math language?

Have you ever dreamed of communicating with computers in the native tongue of mathematics, namely, natural math language when doing math using computers?

I know I have, as a matter of fact, for many years. And this is what this project is really about -- To develop an easy-to-use declarative language that maximally resembles natural math language for defining math problems -- numerical, symbolic, or even logical. Leibniz is a hybrid language that mixes English and symbolic expressions. The syntactic structures it includes (assertion, command, and query) and the ASCII representations of math concepts and entities, are what give rise to its impressive express power.

Indeed, if MOOC is to hold on to the promise of democratizing education better than previous hypes, it must SCALE beyond content delivery, in particular in teacher/students interactions. It's a high order, but Leibniz can help -- question asking and teacher style step-by-step solutions are just the tip of the iceberg of what it can offer!

Just as mathematics is the foundation for physics and engineering, among others, Leibniz language is designed as the base language from which domain languages can be derived with domain-specific lexicons and expanded grammar when necessary.

FAQ

How are you going to monetize this?

We do not have a concrete business model yet, if that is what you are asking. Having that said, however, we do have some ideas on providing learning service to institutions with value-added functionalities such as homework composing and grading in both standardized forms and simple-structured free forms, in adition to provide students assistances essentially as 24/7 private tutors, who can answer questions with well defined context using Leibniz. Our service will send notifications to teachers/TAs when Leibniz fails to give students satisfying answers such that they can get further help. If these type of services are proved saving time for teachers/TAs and allowing them to spend more time with students face-to-face in traditional campus, or improving learning experience of students in MOOC setting, there is money to be earned from these premium services.

It should be noted that the major advantage of our homework service to other available ones such as WeBWork and Pearson's MyMathLab is that the problem set composed using Leibniz can generate solutions in addition to display in natural math notation, which can be pedagogically more valuable than simply provide an immediate "right/wrong" feedback. As far as grading is concerned, which is in general one of the most important re-enforcement step in learning and also the most time consuming part of any teaching (assuming non-standardized form and lecture notes ready) job, the challange and opportunity are equally abundant.

just like natural math language, statements defined in Leibniz carry both typesetting info and semantics (it will become clear after you take a look at our demo). Donald Knuth's TeX language enables typesetting beautiful mathematics, but it does not define any semantics; Namely, it describes how symbols and operators are placed spatially but does not encode anything about what they actually mean (he might have wanted semantics, I'd guess). MathML, on the other hand, defines both typesetting and semantics, but is intended mainly for use by machine since it is an XML-based language. It has gained some support as a W3 standard from software companies such as Maple Soft and Design Science, and Leibniz is to provide a translation layer. The general consensus at least for presentation though is that time-tested TeX/Latex is a more maturer solution (for example, see a review on OpenMath and MathML here ).

Another potential source of revenue is charging technical users who doing routine (not specialized) numerical works on a daily basis with some visualization and storage support. They can access Leibniz through internet, or if they have powerful workstations or multi-core machines in their offices, they have the option to install the software locally and run perhaps from the same browser interface. "Cloud computing" is the current trend, but we see no reason for people to abondon their computing harware they already have. Users who access Leibniz locally will be required to pay a traditional software licence fee.

What is the current status of the project?

Our development is somewhere in between "prototype" and "product", perhaps a little closer to the "product" end. The project was started in about five and half years ago, although the idea was formed during my graduate years at USC. Grammar definition of the language is mostly finalized and considerable implementation has been completed, including core numerical and symbolic libraries, and simple logical programming functionalities that enable Leibniz to perform mathematical reasoning in the context of single variable calculus within the framework of First-Order-Logic. To our best knowledge, formal reasoning in calculus by software is something that nobody has ever tried before despite that reasoning is an essential skill math students are expected to acquire. Our numerical libraries include commonly used recipes such as fast in-core LU and Cholesky decomposition for large sparse matrices in C++, and Grand-Canonical-Ensemble Monte-Carlo.

There are still many tasks remain to be done -- the libraries as they stand now are quite fragile and incomplete.
